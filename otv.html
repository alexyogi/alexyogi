Over Terrain Vehicle Project

Mission Background - Data Extraction

During descent, one of the payloads was damaged and released a hazardous material. However, there is important data stored on the payload that must be extracted and analyzed within 24 hours of the payload landing. The payload’s data is stored on an Arduino that has output ports for data transmission.

Mission Objectives

1. Navigate to within 150 mm of the pylon.

2. Physically connect to both of the pylon output ports.

3. Extract and correctly transmit the duty cycle of the signal square wave to within 5%.

4. Identify and transmit the magnetism of material in the base of the pylon (magnetic or non-magnetic).

5. Navigate completely past the three obstacles.

6. Navigate completely into the destination zone.

Vehicle Requirements

1. Total OTV mass must be no heavier than 3 kg.

2. Pre-built chassis assemblies may perform functions from at most two of the following categories: (1) motors / gearmotors; (2) wheels / tires / treads; (3) suspension; (3) transmission / differential / drivetrain; (5) motor controllers.

3. No building-systems or robotics kits such as Lego, Knex, Vex are allowed.

4. There must be an Aruco tracking marker with dimensions between 50x50mm and 100x100mm with a 15mm white border placed on the top of the vehicle/chassis.

5. No exposed pins, nails, razor blades, or other dangerously sharp objects are permitted on the OTV/chassis.

6. Total as-built replacement cost must be less than $320.

Some challenges for this project include:

1. Making the chassis sturdy while also allowing some give for shock/vibration absorption

2. Supporting the weight of the arm, propulsion, and electronics components without having the chassis bend

3. Allowing a smooth and generous turning radius for the wheels when turning corners

4. Accurately placing the arm over the pylon so it can be lowered

5. Lining up the data connections properly

Design Justification

The mission will utilize a data extraction arm controlled by a single servo motor which will be able to raise and lower the arm into and out of the pylon. The arm will be 3D-printed from PLA plastic and will be attached inside of a 3D-printed vertical rail. The back of the arm will interact with a gear and form a rack-and-pinion system (also made of PLA plastic) that will allow for vertical translation of the arm. The rail will be permanently fixed to the chassis using fasteners which will prevent unnecessary arm motion.

The arm will be placed on the front of the OTV with the arm reaching out over the triangular cutout in the chassis. The arm rails will be placed at the base of the cutout. The mission will be carried out by using a number of data collection sensors. A hall magnetic sensor will be used to determine the magnetism of the puck at the base of the pylon. Copper wiring will be attached to the inside of the arm to contact the data outputs. The wire will transmit the voltage output of the signal and will measure voltage through the Arduino microcontroller. This voltage will be recorded over a given time period and averaged in order to calculate duty cycle. This data can be wirelessly transmitted with a WIFI module to the ENES lab computer.

    • Chassis Design

    I was on the chassis subteam, focusing on the design and prototyping of the vehicle chassis. The chassis needs to provide space for electronics that will be connected to arm and wheels, as well as arduino mount. The design includes four extensions to house four motors to enable four wheel drive. The trapezoidal cutout serves to guide the pylon to the right place and gives the arm space to lower for data extraction. Additionally, the size and shape allow the chassis to balance the weight of the arm and prevent any tipping.

    Final Chassis Design

    This is a CAD rendering for the final design. After starting the prototyping process, we encountered some issues and decided to tweak the original design. We noticed that we needed to add an extra cutout to the triangle in order to allow the extraction arm to operate properly. Additionally, we added railing structures to either side to provide more space for electronics on the chassis. We moved the aruco marker onto a custom cut acrylic sheet, which went on top of the railings. It also helped managed all the wires above the chassis, preventing it from accidentally getting caught or snagged on surrounding objects.

    Other Integral Components

    Large Wheels

    In addition to the unique data extraction mission that our OTV has to execute, it has to complete an obstacle course that includes either going over a hump or under a beam. The data extraction arm already made the vehicle a little tall, so we decided to go over the hump because the vehicle could complete it more confidently. In order to accomodate this, we chose to design our OTV with large, all-terrain tires, giving the vehicle a large ground clearance and the ability to easily traverse the obstable. We ordered these wheels on Amazon.

    Ultrasonic Sensor

    Another section of the course requires the vehicle to manuever around 1-2 obstables. The ultrasonic sensor is placed on the other side of the chassis, so that is doesn't interfere with the data extrcation arm. Therefore, we had the vehicle drive "backwards" for this section. The sensor allows the vehicle to detect objects, and how far away they are as well.

    Motors

    We decided to use this motor from Pololu. We had to also order these 12mm hex wheel adapters to make the motors compatible with the large wheels we chose. These adapters screw onto the motor gearshaft, then connect to the wheels.
